Why this stuff matters

People sometimes wonder why scientists spend years studying fruit flies, worms, or yeast. It might not seem relevant to everyday life. But all life on Earth shares a common ancestor, which means we share genes — even with distant relatives like flies and yeast. A gene that causes cancer in humans often has a counterpart in these organisms, and because they're cheaper and faster to study, scientists can test treatments in them first. The connections extend well beyond medicine into agriculture, conservation, forensics, and more — here are some examples.

Medicine and disease

  • Drug discovery — every new drug must be tested in living organisms before human trials. Phylogeny guides which ones: mice share many disease genes with us because they're fellow mammals, so nearly every approved drug was tested in rodents first. But even zebrafish (vertebrates with see-through eggs that let researchers watch organs form), C. elegans worms (only 302 neurons, yet they share key aging and disease genes with us), and fruit flies are used for early drug screening because they're fast to breed and still share key disease pathways with humans
  • Cancer — two-thirds of genes implicated in human cancers have a counterpart in fruit flies, Nobel Prize-winning research on the cell cycle was done in yeast, and programmed cell death — how cells "decide" to die, which fails in tumors — was discovered in C. elegans worms
  • Vaccine development — WHO selects influenza vaccine strains each year based on phylogenetic analysis of circulating viruses
  • Epidemiology — phylogenetics traced the 2014 Ebola outbreak's spread in detail, was applied on a large scale for SARS-CoV2, and helped track a deadly superbug outbreak at the NIH
  • Food safety — whole-genome phylogenetics links Salmonella outbreaks to specific contaminated food sources
  • Horizontal gene transfer — detecting antibiotic resistance genes that jumped from soil bacteria to pathogens
  • Medical safety — horseshoe crabs (more closely related to spiders than crabs) have blue blood containing a clotting agent used for decades to test vaccines and medical equipment for bacterial contamination
  • Metagenomics — classifying the thousands of microbial species in a gut microbiome sample
  • Comparative genomics — comparing human and mouse genomes reveals conserved regulatory elements

Gene therapies

Many breakthrough medical technologies came from studying other organisms.

  • CRISPR — a bacterial immune system repurposed to precisely edit human genes. The FDA approved the first CRISPR therapy for sickle cell disease, with trials ongoing for cancers and inherited blindness
  • RNA interference — discovered in C. elegans worms (Nobel Prize 2006), now the basis of approved drugs that silence disease-causing genes
  • Antisense oligonucleotides — synthetic molecules that block specific gene messages. Spinraza, the first approved treatment for spinal muscular atrophy, uses this approach
  • Transposon engineering — "jumping genes," first discovered by Barbara McClintock in corn, now harnessed to insert therapeutic genes into human cells. The Sleeping Beauty transposon was reconstructed from ancient fish genomes for use in cancer immunotherapy

Agriculture and environment

  • Agriculture — wild relatives of crops like wheat, rice, and tomato are crossed with cultivated varieties to introduce disease resistance and stress tolerance. Modern efforts go further: pangenome projects now sequence dozens of wild relatives to catalogue the full genetic diversity of a crop species, revealing thousands of genes lost during domestication that breeders can reintroduce
  • Conservation — the tuatara, sole survivor of its order, is prioritized because it represents a unique evolutionary lineage
  • Biogeography — explaining why marsupials dominate Australia but are rare elsewhere
  • Coevolution — figs and fig wasps have coevolved for over 75 million years

Biotechnology and discovery

  • Forensics — phylogenetics of Bacillus anthracis helped trace the 2001 anthrax letter attacks back to a specific lab flask, one of the first major uses of microbial forensics
  • Antibiotics — most antibiotics were originally discovered by screening soil microorganisms and their relatives, including penicillin, streptomycin, and tetracycline
  • Biotechnology — in 1982, engineered E. coli bacteria produced the first recombinant human insulin, replacing animal-sourced insulin for diabetics. The same approach now produces human growth hormone, clotting factors, and many other medicines
  • PCR — Taq polymerase, an enzyme found in heat-tolerant bacteria in Yellowstone hot springs, enabled PCR — a technique that makes millions of copies of a tiny DNA sample so there's enough to detect and analyze, used in nearly every area of modern biology and medicine
  • Ancestral reconstruction — scientists can resurrect ancient proteins to study how they evolved, and engineer more stable versions for drug development and industrial enzymes
  • Convergent evolution — when bats and dolphins independently evolved echolocation using the same gene mutations, it revealed which molecular designs are truly optimal — guiding protein engineering and drug design

Of course, not everything here started with a practical goal. Much of it began as basic curiosity — scientists trying to figure out how the world works. But that's how science operates: open-ended inquiry has a way of producing breakthroughs no one saw coming.
