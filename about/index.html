A note on NCBI Taxonomy

The NCBI Taxonomy database is a classification, not a true phylogeny. It is not a primary source for phylogenetic information, and does not follow a single taxonomic treatise — instead it incorporates knowledge from a variety of sources including published literature, web-based databases, and outside experts. As a result, the relationships it describes may not always reflect the latest phylogenetic understanding. See the NCBI Taxonomy Help for more details on how the database is curated.

Why phylogeny and taxonomy matter

Phylogeny — the evolutionary relationships among species — underpins much of biology. Taxonomy provides the shared naming system that makes this work: it organizes life's diversity while phylogeny reveals the evolutionary history behind it.

Applied sciences

  • Drug discovery — picking model organisms closest to humans
  • Agriculture — finding wild relatives for crop breeding and identifying biological pest control agents
  • Vaccine development — tracking how viral strains are related to design effective vaccines
  • Epidemiology — tracking pathogen evolution, spread, and antibiotic resistance
  • Food safety — tracing outbreaks of foodborne pathogens back to their source
  • Conservation — prioritizing evolutionarily unique lineages
  • Invasive species — identifying origins and predicting which relatives might also become invasive
  • Forensics — linking biological evidence to its source
  • Bioprospecting — screening relatives of known producers for useful compounds

Genomics and molecular biology

  • Comparative genomics — predicting gene function from related species
  • Ancestral reconstruction — inferring ancient proteins and traits
  • Horizontal gene transfer — detecting genes that jumped between species
  • Metagenomics — classifying organisms in environmental DNA samples

Ecology and evolution

  • Biogeography — explaining species distributions across the globe
  • Coevolution — studying reciprocal adaptations between hosts and parasites
  • Trait evolution — reconstructing when traits like flight, venom, or photosynthesis arose or were lost
  • Convergent evolution — identifying traits that evolved independently in unrelated lineages
